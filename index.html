San Francisco Crime Data Story

Analyzing Shifts in San Francisco Property Crime (2018–2025)

In this project, we explored how different types of property crime trended after 2019. While total recorded incidents dropped, categories like burglary and motor vehicle theft did not follow the same path as larceny. By tracking district level data, we mapped how the geographic distribution of larceny shifted across SFPD districts over time.

Key Metrics at a Glance

  • Larceny/Theft (2019 to 2025): -58.4%
  • Burglary (2019 to 2020): +51.8%
  • Motor Vehicle Theft (2019 to 2023): +65.9%
  • Largest share shifts (2019 to 2025): SOUTHERN (+6.0 pp), CENTRAL (-6.0 pp)

Scope and Boundaries

For this project, we derived all counts from merged SFPD incident records (reproducible via our build_assignment2_assets.py script). We limited our scope to full years from 2018 to 2025, with normalized district names restricted to the 10 official SFPD districts. In our analysis, "property crime" specifically refers to Larceny/Theft, Burglary, and Motor Vehicle Theft.

1. Divergence Across Property Crime Categories

In our analysis of the filtered citywide data, we found that the total volume of property crime fell dramatically between 2019 and 2025, dropping from 76,695 incidents to 41,577 (a 45.7% decrease). However, this aggregate number hides three vastly different trajectories.

We observed that Larceny/Theft dropped quickly during the initial disruption period. Conversely, Burglary and Motor Vehicle Theft rose significantly above their 2019 baselines before eventually tapering off.

Using an index scale allowed us to make this split directly comparable. In 2020, Larceny/Theft sat at 63.28 (relative to its 2019 baseline of 100), while Burglary spiked to 151.88, and Motor Vehicle Theft hit 139.30. By 2023, Larceny remained suppressed (79.65), while Motor Vehicle Theft reached its peak at 165.98.

Because Larceny/Theft historically dominates these categories in sheer volume (accounting for 46,754 incidents in 2019 compared to roughly 5,000 for the others), we chose to focus the spatial portion of our analysis strictly on Larceny. High-volume categories provide a much clearer, more reliable signal for measuring district-level composition shifts.

2. Shifting Geographic Composition of Larceny

When comparing the endpoints (2019 versus 2025), our data reveals a massive reallocation of where larceny occurs within the city. To measure this, we calculated "district share"—the fraction of citywide larceny that happened in a specific district. This isolates the geographic composition from the overall drop in crime volume.

In 2019, the CENTRAL district vastly exceeded the SOUTHERN district in its share of larceny by nearly 10 percentage points (20.9% vs 11.0%). By 2025, we found that this ordering entirely flipped. SOUTHERN grew to exceed CENTRAL, holding 17.1% of the city's share compared to CENTRAL's 14.9%.

We want to emphasize that a gain in share does not mean an increase in absolute crime. For example, while the SOUTHERN district gained the largest share (+6.0 pp), its absolute larceny count still declined from 5,122 incidents to 3,261. Its share grew simply because crime there shrank much slower than in the rest of the city.

We tracked this geographic redistribution across the entire city. NORTHERN's share dropped by 3.1 pp, while residential-heavy districts like TARAVAL and MISSION saw their relative shares rise by 2.7 pp and 1.8 pp, respectively.

3. Tracing the Timelines of Disruption

While comparing 2019 to 2025 summarizes the final outcome, we utilized monthly trajectories to reveal how those endpoints were reached. Districts ending up with similar shares in 2025 did not necessarily follow the same path to get there.

To quantify the immediate shock of the pandemic period, we compared each district's average monthly share during the 2020-2021 window against its 2019 monthly average. The largest absolute shifts occurred in CENTRAL (-2.1 pp), INGLESIDE (+2.1 pp), and TENDERLOIN (-2.0 pp).

Our findings indicate that these figures represent sustained average differences over many months rather than brief single-month spikes. This suggests that the geographic shift in property crime was a prolonged structural change rather than a momentary glitch in the data.

Conclusion

Cross-examining these three perspectives allowed us to establish three durable results. First, property crime categories diverged sharply after 2019 and have only partially reconverged by 2025. Second, the geographic composition of Larceny heavily rotated across districts, with SOUTHERN absorbing the largest relative increase in share and CENTRAL experiencing the steepest drop.

Ultimately, our project highlights the importance of separating absolute counts from compositional shares. As demonstrated by the SOUTHERN district, an area can take on a larger burden of the city's overall crime share even while its local incident count is actively falling. These records describe significant changes in reporting patterns, geographic load, and timing, laying a factual groundwork for understanding San Francisco's shifting crime landscape.
